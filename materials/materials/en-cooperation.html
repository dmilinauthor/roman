THE SECOND CALL

DMITRY ILYIN

INTELLECTUAL SPY NOIR · DECEMBER 1946 — MAY 1948

Prologue

By December 1946, Europe had become a vast espionage proving ground where the old rules of the game had finally ceased to apply. The era of the “aristocrats of the spirit”, for whom espionage was a matter of honour, was being displaced by a new reality — the technocratic power of corporations and shadow organisations. The old school was fading, giving way to cold calculation, in which a man was merely a cog in the system and secret information a mere line in an asset ledger.

Post-war Switzerland found itself at the epicentre of this transformation: its cantons had become a colossal filter for the gold reserves and capital being channelled into Latin America. The conclusion of the Nuremberg trials and the signing of the Paris Peace Treaties had sealed the division of Europe, yet they had merely exposed the rift that would soon be called the Cold War.

CENTRE.

NIGHT

Bern, 12 December 1946, 23:07. (Junkerngasse 63)

He had begun waiting five cigars earlier. The ash never fell — he removed it at the very last moment, with two short movements of his fingers. Without haste. Without a glance. Just as he had once removed men from their posts, struck countries from maps, and struck questions from the agenda. He was always ahead. By one step. By two. By a lifetime.

The study was cold. Not out of forgetfulness, but conviction. In December he never turned on the heating. The cold disciplined thought. The cold did not allow memory to relax its guard. The wooden panelling, which still remembered the era of great upheavals, absorbed this cold, becoming almost stone-like. Every detail in this study had been thought out to the smallest degree.

The slender second hand of the Patek Philippe cut time into identical slices almost silently. He had not looked at his watch once that evening, yet he knew every minute. The music had ended. The gramophone continued to turn, but the needle merely scratched the groove.

Then came Richter’s turn. Allegro non troppo e molto maestoso.

The heavy opening chords slowly filled the study with their weight. Richter never played for the listener — he played for eternity. He needed a little more time to prepare for what was to come.

On the desk lay five short cigar stubs — identical, neat, almost symmetrical. He always extinguished his cigar before the fire reached his fingers. That was the right way. That was always the way. His entire life consisted of this: not waiting for the flame to burn him, but deciding himself when it was time to stop.

The telephone remained silent. He opened the revolver cylinder. Checked it once more, though there was nothing to check. Five empty chambers. One loaded. The metallic click sounded unexpectedly loud, cutting through the music. Now he was ready.

Only the second call remained.

Walter entered quietly. But the old floorboards still responded with a faint vibration beneath the carpet. They were two hundred and sixty-three years old, and in that time they had learned to distinguish people by the weight of their tread. Walter was soaked to the bone.

Steam rose from his wet overcoat and broad shoulders. The rain seemed still to cling to him, unwilling to let go.

He said nothing. He merely nodded. The man at the desk did not reply. He did not even move. The light of the green lamp lay upon his face as motionless as upon bronze. Walter stood for another second, as if expecting something, then turned and left. The floorboards trembled once more.

The door closed. The house grew calm.

And only then did he slowly open the revolver cylinder. Five empty. One inside. For several seconds he stared at the cartridge as if seeing it for the first time.

The second call had not sounded here. It had sounded where it was meant to.

Tomorrow the newspapers would appear as usual. The radio would speak in ordinary voices. Another nine and a half cigars, and the city would awaken.

He switched off the lamp. And darkness immediately took its place, closing this episode forever.
